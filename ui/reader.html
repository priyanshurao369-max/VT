The star was dying, but it wasn't doing it quietly. It was a slow, agonizing process that painted the cosmos in hues of violent violet and deep, bruised crimson. I watched from the observation deck, my breath hitching as the sensors spiked.

"We have approximately four minutes until the first wave hits," Rocky said. His voice, translated through the synth-link, sounded strangely melodic today. He was tapping his claws against the hull, a rhythmic sound that reminded me of rain on a tin roof back in California.

The calculations were perfect, yet the sheer scale of the phenomenon made my human mind recoil in primal fear of the unknown. I adjusted the dial on the magnetic shield, feeling the subtle vibration of the ship's engines as they struggled to maintain a stable orbit. Every second felt like an eternity.

If we failed here, it wasn't just us. It was everyone. The Earth would freeze, a silent snowball spinning through a lightless void. I thought of the children back in my classroom, their faces bright with curiosity, unaware that their former teacher was currently staring into the eye of a solar god.

"Ready?" I asked.

"Question: Is 'ready' a state of mind or a physical preparedness?" Rocky replied.
